    There are several well-known frameworks that describe progression from novice to mastery and ultimately teaching others. They come from education, psychology, military training, martial arts, and professional development. They are complementary rather than competing.

    1. Bloom's Taxonomy (Most Common in Education)¶

    Bloom's Taxonomy describes increasing cognitive mastery:

    1. Remember – Recall facts
    2. Understand – Explain concepts
    3. Apply – Use knowledge in practice
    4. Analyze – Understand relationships and tradeoffs
    5. Evaluate – Judge alternatives and make decisions
    6. Create – Build new solutions, models, or ideas

    A modern AI/Product Builder interpretation:

    Level Example
    Remember Knows what Kubernetes is
    Understand Explains Kubernetes concepts
    Apply Deploys applications
    Analyze Diagnoses cluster issues
    Evaluate Chooses between EKS, GKE, AKS
    Create Designs a new platform architecture

    Teaching usually begins around Evaluate and becomes strongest at Create.

    2. Dreyfus Model of Skill Acquisition¶

    Widely used in software engineering, medicine, aviation, and military training.

    1. Novice¶

    Follows rules exactly.

    "Tell me the steps."

    2. Advanced Beginner¶

    Recognizes patterns.

    "I've seen this before."

    3. Competent¶

    Plans and prioritizes.

    "I can manage a project."

    4. Proficient¶

    Sees systems and context.

    "I know which approach fits this situation."

    5. Expert¶

    Operates intuitively.

    "I can immediately spot what's wrong."

    Many organizations stop here.

    6. Master (Unofficial Extension)¶

    Creates new approaches.

    Invents frameworks. Defines best practices. Writes books.

    7. Teacher¶

    Transfers mastery to others.

    Builds other experts.

    3. Conscious Competence Model¶

    Very useful for coaching and leadership development.

    Unconscious Incompetence¶

    You don't know what you don't know.

    Conscious Incompetence¶

    You know what you're missing.

    Conscious Competence¶

    You can perform deliberately.

    Unconscious Competence¶

    Skill becomes automatic.

    Reflective Mastery (often added)¶

    You can explain why you do what you do.

    Teaching generally requires this final step.

    4. Apprenticeship Model¶

    One of the oldest frameworks.

    Observe¶

    Watch experts.

    Assist¶

    Help experts.

    Perform¶

    Work independently.

    Lead¶

    Guide others.

    Teach¶

    Train apprentices.

    This is how many crafts, trades, and martial arts operate.

    5. Shu-Ha-Ri (Japanese Mastery Model)¶

    Popular in software craftsmanship and Lean circles.

    Shu (Obey)¶

    Learn and follow rules.

    Ha (Detach)¶

    Understand when rules can be bent.

    Ri (Transcend)¶

    Create your own approaches.

    Teaching usually occurs at Ri.

    Shu-Ha-Ri

    6. Feynman Learning Loop¶

    Popular among engineers.

    Learn¶

    Study the topic.

    Explain¶

    Teach simply.

    Identify Gaps¶

    Find weaknesses.

    Refine¶

    Improve understanding.

    Teach Again¶

    Repeat.

    Key idea:

    If you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it well enough.

    Associated with Richard Feynman.

    7. Knowledge → Wisdom Pyramid (DIKW)¶

    A strategic leadership model.

    Data¶

    Raw facts.

    Information¶

    Organized facts.

    Knowledge¶

    Ability to use information.

    Understanding¶

    Why things work.

    Wisdom¶

    Knowing what should be done.

    Teaching¶

    Helping others acquire wisdom.

    This is common among executives and thought leaders.

    Product Builder / CPTO Progression¶

    Given your interest in Product Management, Product Builders, and CPTO development, I would combine several frameworks into a practical ladder:

    Stage Focus Evidence
    Learner Knowledge Courses, books
    Practitioner Application Individual contributor
    Builder Creation Ships products
    Expert Judgment Solves hard problems
    Architect Systems Thinking Designs organizations/platforms
    Leader Multiplication Leads teams
    Teacher Capability Building Develops others
    Thought Leader Industry Impact Frameworks, books, talks
    Legacy Builder Institution Building Creates enduring organizations

    For example, your work on AgentPlexus, capability-stack-spec, PRISM, and multispec is already operating beyond "Expert" and into Architect and Thought Leader territory because you're creating frameworks that others can adopt, not just applying existing ones.

    A concise version that many executives use is:

    Learn → Do → Lead → Teach → Transform

    where:

    • Learn = acquire knowledge
    • Do = apply it
    • Lead = scale it through teams
    • Teach = create other leaders
    • Transform = change the industry through new frameworks, standards, or institutions

    That progression maps surprisingly well to careers that evolve from engineer → product leader → founder → industry thought leader.

    Yes. Most learning and mastery frameworks are describing the same underlying journey from different perspectives.

    If you normalize them, they tend to converge on a common pattern:

    Universal Stage Bloom Dreyfus Conscious Competence Shu-Ha-Ri Apprenticeship
    Exposure Remember Novice Unconscious Incompetence Shu Observe
    Understanding Understand Advanced Beginner Conscious Incompetence Shu Assist
    Application Apply Competent Conscious Competence Shu/Ha Perform
    Judgment Analyze/Evaluate Proficient Conscious Competence Ha Lead
    Mastery Create Expert/Master Unconscious Competence Ri Master
    Multiplication Teach/Create Teacher Reflective Mastery Ri Teach

    The names differ, but the structure is remarkably consistent.

    The Underlying Pattern¶

    Most frameworks move through six transitions:

    1. Knowledge Acquisition¶

    "What is this?"

    You learn vocabulary, concepts, and rules.

    Examples:

    • Learn Kubernetes objects
    • Learn accounting principles
    • Learn product management terminology

    2. Comprehension¶

    "Why does this work?"

    You begin building mental models.

    Examples:

    • Understand why Kubernetes uses controllers
    • Understand double-entry accounting
    • Understand customer discovery

    3. Application¶

    "Can I actually do it?"

    You start producing results.

    Examples:

    • Deploy a cluster
    • Create a financial model
    • Ship a feature

    4. Judgment¶

    "Which approach should I use?"

    This is where many people plateau.

    Knowledge becomes decision-making.

    Examples:

    • Choose EKS vs self-managed Kubernetes
    • Decide between product strategies
    • Select the right organizational structure

    5. Creation¶

    "Can I create something new?"

    You stop consuming frameworks and start building them.

    Examples:

    • Create a platform architecture
    • Develop a methodology
    • Invent a process

    This is where experts become architects.

    6. Multiplication¶

    "Can I create other experts?"

    This is the highest level in most mastery models.

    Examples:

    • Mentoring
    • Teaching
    • Building organizations
    • Writing books
    • Creating standards

    At this stage the output is no longer products or projects.

    The output is people and institutions.

    Why Teaching Appears Near the Top¶

    Teaching forces several difficult capabilities simultaneously:

    1. Deep understanding
    2. Simplification
    3. Pattern recognition
    4. Communication
    5. Empathy
    6. Knowledge organization

    This is why many people say:

    You don't truly understand something until you can teach it.

    Teaching exposes hidden gaps that day-to-day execution can hide.

    A More General Meta-Framework¶

    Across nearly every field—engineering, medicine, military, athletics, music, leadership—you can reduce the progression to:

    Know → Do → Decide → Create → Teach

    or:

    Consumer → Practitioner → Expert → Architect → Teacher

    This is why a senior engineer, surgeon, military commander, professor, and product executive often seem to follow similar career arcs despite working in completely different domains.

    An Interesting Extension: Teaching Is Not the End¶

    Many frameworks stop at teaching, but leadership and organizational development often add another level:

    Level Output
    Practitioner Work
    Expert Better Work
    Teacher Better People
    Leader Better Teams
    Builder Better Organizations
    Visionary Better Industries

    For example:

    • Richard Feynman was a teacher.
    • Peter Drucker influenced management as a field.
    • W. Edwards Deming changed how organizations think about quality.
    • Jeff Bezos institutionalized ideas like Working Backwards and PR/FAQ.

    Those people weren't just teaching individuals. They created frameworks that outlived them.

    That's why many leadership scholars distinguish between:

    Mastery → Teaching → Thought Leadership → Institution Building

    The last two stages are where people stop transferring knowledge and start shaping how entire professions think and operate.

    Yes. I think AI is causing something more profound than simply making people more productive. It is changing the unit of execution inside organizations.

    Historically, organizations existed because no single person could reasonably perform all the required functions.

    The Traditional Specialization Era¶

    In a SaaS company, work was divided:

    Function Typical Role
    Vision Founder
    Product PM
    UX Designer
    Frontend Engineer
    Backend Engineer
    QA Tester
    DevOps SRE
    Security Security Engineer
    Analytics Data Analyst
    Documentation Technical Writer
    Marketing Product Marketing

    This specialization was economically necessary.

    A founder might understand all these areas, but couldn't execute them all.

    The Solo Founder Model¶

    The classic solo founder was essentially a compressed company.

    Think of people like:

    • Paul Graham
    • Jason Fried
    • Pieter Levels

    They operated as:

    Founder
  ├─ Product
  ├─ Engineering
  ├─ UX
  ├─ Marketing
  └─ Operations

    The limitation wasn't knowledge.

    The limitation was execution bandwidth.

    AI Changes the Constraint¶

    For the first time, execution capacity is becoming partially automatable.

    A modern Product Builder can increasingly delegate work to:

    • Coding agents
    • Research agents
    • QA agents
    • Design agents
    • Documentation agents
    • Analytics agents

    The human remains accountable.

    The AI performs portions of execution.

    The constraint shifts from:

    "Can I do all the work?"

    to

    "Can I direct all the work?"

    Responsibility Collapse¶

    I think "responsibility collapse" is a useful term.

    Historically:

    1 Product Manager
5 Engineers
2 QA
1 Designer
1 SRE

    Now we increasingly see:

    1 Product Builder
+
AI agents

    The responsibilities remain.

    The number of humans decreases.

    The human becomes a coordinator of capabilities.

    This is similar to how cloud computing collapsed infrastructure roles.

    You still need:

    • Networking
    • Compute
    • Storage
    • Security

    But fewer humans provision servers manually.

    Product Builder as the Enterprise Version of a Founder¶

    A Product Builder is essentially a founder without ownership.

    A founder owns:

    • Vision
    • Roadmap
    • Delivery
    • Operations
    • Revenue

    A Product Builder increasingly owns:

    • Vision
    • Roadmap
    • Prototype
    • Production
    • Operations

    inside a larger organization.

    This is why the role feels different from traditional Product Management.

    Traditional PMs optimize decisions.

    Product Builders optimize outcomes.

    Why CPTO Emerges Naturally¶

    The traditional executive structure separated:

    CPO
  owns what

CTO
  owns how

    This separation made sense when:

    • Product teams were large
    • Engineering teams were large
    • Communication costs were high

    AI reduces coordination costs.

    As a result, product and engineering increasingly converge.

    A CPTO becomes:

    Owns:
- Vision
- Product Strategy
- Architecture
- Delivery
- Operations
- AI leverage

    This looks surprisingly similar to what founders have always done.

    A New Mastery Ladder¶

    Historically:

    Engineer
→ Senior Engineer
→ Architect
→ CTO

    or

    PM
→ Senior PM
→ Director
→ VP Product
→ CPO

    AI is creating a new path:

    Maker
→ Builder
→ Product Builder
→ Platform Builder
→ CPTO
→ Founder

    Notice that this path is less function-specific.

    It is centered on ownership.

    The Capability Compression Curve¶

    A useful way to think about it:

    Industrial Era¶

    One person performed one function.

    Information Era¶

    One person managed one function.

    AI Era¶

    One person orchestrates many functions.

    The progression becomes:

    Specialist
→ Generalist
→ Builder
→ Multiplier

    where the multiplier may be multiplying through:

    • Humans
    • AI agents
    • Platforms
    • Automation

    or all three.

    The Highest Stage: Institution Builder¶

    This is where the framework extends beyond Product Builder and CPTO.

    Stage Primary Output
    Engineer Code
    PM Decisions
    Product Builder Products
    Founder Companies
    CPTO Product Organizations
    Institution Builder Ecosystems

    Examples:

    • Steve Jobs built products and a company.
    • Jeff Bezos built a company and operating mechanisms.
    • Elon Musk builds companies and industrial ecosystems.

    The interesting implication is that AI may not primarily eliminate jobs. Instead, it may allow more people to move up the ladder:

    Learn
→ Do
→ Build
→ Lead
→ Teach
→ Multiply
→ Build Institutions

    In that world, the Product Builder is not replacing the PM.

    The Product Builder becomes the modern equivalent of what a capable solo founder could historically do, while the CPTO becomes the executive responsible for enabling hundreds of Product Builders across an enterprise. That's why your ProductBuildersHQ concept resonates with current trends: it is describing a role that sits between traditional product management and traditional entrepreneurship, enabled by AI-driven capability compression.
